What Creates Value?

Pinning down a definition precise enough to evaluate a real investment decision

Most managers can describe what their organization sells. Far fewer can describe, precisely, when their organization is creating value — and that gap costs real money.

The Working Definition

Inputs ($x)
→ transform →
Outputs ($x + $v)
=
Value Created ($v)

Economic value is generated when worthwhile things that did not exist before are created. When inputs worth $x are transformed into outputs that customers are willing to pay $x plus $v for, value of $v has been created.

That $v is the whole game. It's not revenue. It's not profit. It's the gap between what went in and what the market will pay for what came out.

💼 Manager implication When you evaluate any new initiative, ask: what is the $v, and who decided it's worth that much? If you can't answer both halves, you don't have a value-creation story — you have an activity.

Value creation answers “how much value exists.” Added value answers a sharper question: how much of that value would disappear if your firm weren't in the picture?

The Definition

A firm's added value is the portion of total value created that would be lost if the firm did not take part in the exchange. It depends heavily on the effects of existing competition.

AsksHow much value exists in this exchange?How much of it would vanish without this firm?
Depends onThe transformation itselfCompetition & substitutability

⚠ The competition trap High value creation does not guarantee high added value. If ten competitors could create the same value, your added value approaches zero — even while value creation stays exactly the same.

This is the example to remember for the rest of the semester — the specialty cake.

🍰

You're the only baker

added value = $8

Raw ingredients are worth $17. Because you're the only one who knows the secret recipe, the finished cake sells for $25. If you decide not to bake it, none of that $8 in new value is realized — we're left with just $17 in raw resources. All $8 of value created is also your added value.

👥

Ten bakers know the recipe

added value → $0

The cake still creates $8 of value — but your added value collapses, because customers now have options. If you walk away, a competitor simply bakes the cake instead. Nothing is lost.

💡 Key insight Competition doesn't change the value created. It changes how much of it you can keep.
Knowledge Check 1

A regional coffee chain develops a proprietary roasting process that customers love, generating $4 of value per bag over raw ingredient cost. A national competitor reverse-engineers a nearly identical process within six months. What happens to the chain's added value?

Two Ways to Create New Value

The two levers managers actually have, and the ground rules underneath both

Raise Customer Willingness to Pay

The first lever works on the customer side of the transaction. The strategy: drive CWP as high as you possibly can, then price low enough that customers feel like they're capturing significant value when they buy — so they buy in volume (Jobs, 2010).

📱

The 2010 iPad

Apple priced the original iPad well below what analysts expected, even though willingness to pay was sky-high. The perceived gap between value and price — not the price itself — is what drove adoption at scale.

💼 Manager lens This is a balancing act, not a maximization problem. Maximize price and you maximize margin per unit — and kill volume.

Lower Supplier Opportunity Cost

The second lever works on the supplier side: lower what it costs your suppliers to do business with you, and capture part of that savings as added value.

📦

Walmart's Continuous Replenishment

By integrating directly with supplier inventory systems, Walmart lowers suppliers' own cost of doing business with them — and captures some of that savings rather than leaving it on the table.

💼 Manager lens Same value-creation logic, opposite direction. CWP pulls value from the customer side; SOC pushes efficiency into the supplier side.

Four Ground Rules

  • ◆ Value is in the eye of the customer — not in your cost structure, not in your effort.
  • ◆ CWP is not the same as price — willingness to pay is a ceiling; price is a choice you make below it.
  • ◆ Value can be tangible or intangible — brand trust, convenience, and status are real value with no physical component.
  • ◆ Creation of value is not the same as appropriation — you can create enormous value and capture none of it.
✅ Value vs. appropriation Competitive advantage and added value are nearly the same question asked two ways. Firms must actively defend their share of value created, or competitors — and customers, and suppliers — will capture it instead. Protect appropriation, not just creation.

Section 3 of 7

Strategic vs. Tactical IS

Most systems in your organization are not strategic. Here's the test that tells you which ones are.

Strategic information systems are not defined by their functionality or the organizational function they support. They're defined by their objectives and the purpose they serve. The same piece of software can be tactical in one firm and strategic in another, depending on what it's used to accomplish.

🔑 The test A strategic IS positions the firm to capture value that rivals can't easily replicate — an IT-dependent activity system competitors can't easily copy. Hold every “strategic” claim to that bar.

Tactical information systems don't position the firm to create added value — but they're still crucial, often mission-critical, for operations. No modern organization runs without ERP, CRM, supply chain systems, e-mail, or basic productivity software.

⚠ The budget trap Implementing or upgrading tactical systems will not create competitive advantage. You need them. They will not differentiate you. Don't confuse “necessary” with “strategic.”

IT-dependent strategic initiatives are not simply a piece of software, a digital device, or a program that might generate an advantage until someone copies it. They're a configuration of an entire activity system — people, process, and technology together — dependent on IT at its core, built specifically to create and appropriate economic value.

💼 Manager lens The test isn't “is the technology innovative.” The test is: does it create added value? IT investments only pay off when they're part of a larger, cohesive system design — not as a standalone purchase.

Five Forces — A Digital Value Lens

Porter's framework, asked one new question: where can IT change the answer?

Industry analysis using Porter's five forces framework (Porter, 1980) lets managers ask one question, five different ways: can the use of IT change this force in our favor?

🚧

Threat of New Entrants

Raise the barrier

Can IT create or increase barriers to entry? Strong IT infrastructure can make it costlier for new rivals to compete.

🔁

Threat of Substitutes

Lock in unique value

Can IT reduce the appeal or use of substitute products or services? Locking in unique value lowers the threat.

🛒

Bargaining Power of Buyers

Reduce buyer leverage

Can IT decrease buyers' bargaining power? Switching costs and personalization both reduce buyer leverage.

🏭

Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Widen sourcing options

Can IT decrease suppliers' bargaining power? Integrated procurement systems widen sourcing options and shift leverage back to you.

🔑 Manager insight Industry analysis helps managers decide whether to enter an industry — and how to increase its appeal once they're already in it.

The fifth force gets its own tab because it's the one most directly about IT-enabled competition: can IT change the basis of competition itself?

💰

Cost Leadership

IT can lower your costs faster than rivals can match.

✨

Differentiation

Superior IT-enabled service or experience becomes a moat.

🔒

Switching Costs

Lock-in raises the cost of a customer leaving for a competitor.

⚡

Speed to Market

Faster IT-enabled innovation cycles outpace analog-speed rivals.

Value Chain, Value Network & CSLC

Three lenses for finding exactly where value is created, exchanged, and delivered

Managers use value chain analysis to outperform competitors by using IS to create added value and competitive advantage.

💰

Primary Activities

Revenue generators

The firm's actions directly related to value creation.

🛠

Support Activities

Cost centers

Not directly tied to value creation, but necessary to enable it.

💼 Manager's goal Use IS to add value at each link in the chain — not just one. Advantage: maps value creation step by step. Disadvantage: treats the firm in isolation.

The firm's own value chain doesn't exist in isolation — it sits inside a larger network that includes suppliers upstream and customers downstream.

The points of contact between these separate value chains are called linkages, and they're prime spots to deploy IT-dependent strategic initiatives — because that's where one firm's output becomes another firm's input.

⚠ Be careful Never apply this model “as is.” Adapt it to your firm and industry using your in-depth knowledge of that business — the textbook example is a lodging stay; yours will look different.

The idea that a firm can use IS to create value through superior customer service has gotten new energy from the internet and from connected devices as viable business tools.

As more organizations build direct relationships with customers, the potential for new value creation at the “front of the house” — the point of direct customer contact — has grown substantially.

🔑 The question CSLC asks Where, in the customer's actual experience with us, can IT create value they'll notice and pay for?

The Virtual Value Chain & The Value Matrix

Treating information itself as a value-creating resource — and combining it with the physical value chain

Information is no longer just a support resource for the physical value chain — it can itself be treated as an input of a productive transformation process. Information is no longer just a record of what happened. It's a raw material.

📊

1. Gather

Collect raw data from operations, transactions, and customer touchpoints across the firm.

📁

2. Organize

Classify and structure that raw data into categories ready for analysis.

🎯

3. Select

Filter for the data most relevant to the question or opportunity in front of you.

🧠

4. Synthesize

Combine and analyze the selected data to uncover patterns and new insights.

🚀

5. Distribute

Deliver those insights, new processes, products, or services to the people who need them.

By combining the physical value chain and the virtual value chain, GMs and FMs get a cohesive framework for seeking and exploiting opportunities for IT-dependent strategic initiatives.

Section 7 of 7 · Synthesis

Key Takeaways & Self-Assessment

Five principles that should change how you approach IS decisions as a manager

🔑 Your diagnostic toolkit Any value-creation question you face in your career — whether you're the manager, the sponsor, or the subject matter expert — can be analyzed through one or more of these five lenses.
01

Value does not equal revenue

Value creation is a transformation; added value is what would be lost if the firm exited the exchange. Only added value can be appropriated as profit. Revenue can grow while added value shrinks — watch for that.

02

Strategic does not equal necessary

ERP, CRM, and e-mail are vital, but tactical. Strategic IS is defined by its ability to create added value, not by its functionality. Don't let a vendor's feature list convince you something is strategic.

03

The five forces reframe competition

IT can raise entry barriers, blunt substitutes, and shift bargaining power — the same five forces you may already know, seen through a digital lens.

04

Linkages are where value hides

The points of contact between a firm's value chain and its suppliers and customers are prime spots for IT-dependent strategic initiatives. Look at the seams, not just the departments.

05

Data is itself a resource

The virtual value chain treats information as an input, not just a record. Gather, organize, select, synthesize, and distribute to create new value.
